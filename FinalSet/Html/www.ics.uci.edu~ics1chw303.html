  1. What is the World-Wide Web?

     The World-Wide Web(aka WWW or W3) is a network of information accessible world-wide through the Internet. WWW was developed at CERN(the European Particle Physics Laboratory in Geneva, Switzerland as a means for computer users from remote sites to access a variety of information with a consistent user interface. To view a Web document, one needs a Web browser(e.g. Mosaic, Netscape, etc...). Web documents are written in HTML(Hypertext Markup Language). Web documents contains links--that is, if one clicks on the highlighted link--it takes one to another document that is identified by a URL (Uniform Resource Locators). Many interesting things can be found on the Web by subject.

  2. What is HTML?

     Web documents are written in HTML(Hypertext Markup Language). An HTML document contains high-lighted links which if one clicks on it--can take them to a different document or other resources on the Internet (via URL's within the link). An HTML document is structured so that any Web browser (e.g. Mosaic, Netscape, etc...) can read it and display it within the capabilities of the computer system that one is working on. Using HTML, one concentrates on the document's structure--NOT on the document's appearance--this enables developers of Web documents to express their ideas and not have to worry about whether any Web browser will be able to interpret and display their pages. To see what an HTML document looks like, if you are using Mosaic, go to the File option and click on View Source. As one can see, < and > dominate the document. These are called tags(sequences of characters that start with < and end with >). NCSA's Beginner's Guide to HTML has some excellent information on how to construct an HTML doucment. Another good reference on HTML documentation is Univeristy of Toronto's HTML Documentation.

  3. What is Mathematics?

     Mathematics is the language of science without which we could not possibly have our present comfort and progress in civilization. Just as physics describes the the phenomena of nature--mathematics is the language which is used to described the phenomena. What other language in the world is understood universally? It is Mathematics--2 + 2 =4 is understood by everybody worldwide. Mathematics is a hallowed established science. Mathematics has been studied world-wide since ancient times. Look at history of mathematics by region for interesting contributions in mathematics from mathematicians world-wide since the beginning of time. The Internet Guide for Mathematics Problems has some Putnam exam questions along with the answers.

  5. How does one become a successful graduate student?

     For any of you who are considering continuing your education with a graduate degree should take a look at Information for Graduate Students. This Web page is basically for computer science graduate students but it has some relevant and worthwhile topics to consider--including Piroz Mohseni's A Guide for Applying to Graduate Schools. One should also research potential graduate schools and their programs--a good place to start is College and University Home Pages. Some quick tips:

    • Start the process early--preferably when you are a sophomore or junior in undergraduate study.
    • Investigate some schools that you are interested in applying to--that way you can structure your undergraduate course work so as to meet their requirements.
    • Prepare for the GRE(Graduate Record Exam). Everyone must take the General GRE--some schools may require you to take the Subject GRE also-- check the programs that you are interested in applying to.
    • Try to maintain a good GPA. Most schools require that you have at least a 3.0 to apply.
    • Get to know AT LEAST THREE or FOUR professors reasonably well, i.e., attend lectures regularly, attend their office hours, and do reasonably well in their class. This is important since most graduate programs require three letters of recommendations--at least two must be from academia.
    • Write your Statement of Purpose AT LEAST TWO MONTHS IN ADVANCE. You should also have at least two people to proofread and evaluate your Statement.
    • When you ask letters of reference from your professors--make sure you include the necessary forms, a copy of your current transcript, a copy of your Statement of Purpose, and stamped envelopes for the schools that you are applying to. MOST IMPORTANTLY, WRITE A THANK-YOU LETTER TO THE PROFESSOR--HEY, THEY'RE DOING YOU A FAVOR!!
    • Be aware of deadlines for the schools that you are applying to!!
