Throughout time there has been many influential African-American people and Rosa Parks is one of them. Rosa maiden name was Rosa Loise McCauley. She was born in Tuskegee, Alabama on February 4, 1913.

Growing up Rosa went through a lot of trials and tribulations. Her father moved out on her when she was two years old and left her, her mother Leona, and her brother Sylvester to move in with her grandparents. Her grandparents and her mother were all in bad health so Rosa took over the motherly chores such as the cooking, cleaning, shopping, and sewing. At age 11 Rosa went to live with her aunt in Montgomery, where she enrolled in the Montgomery Industrial School for Girls. They didn't have enough money to pay for tuition so Rosa cleaned the classrooms after school to compensate for the inadequate funds.

In 1943 Rosa joined the NAACP where she helped register black voters. She was always into the upbringing of African-Americans because she encouraged other blacks to get involved in the Civil Rights Movement and this was quite evident from what happened to her in 1955 on a Montgomery bus. She was riding on the bus when it came to a stop and a white man came on the bus. While sitting in her seat and minding her on business, the white man tells her to get out of her seat and move to the back of the bus. Rosa said "No" of course but it didn't stop there. The bus driver also told her to get out of her seat but she still didn't move and the bus driver threatned to call the authorities, but nothing changed. The police later came and arrested Rosa. She had to go to court and she eventually paid $14 to be let go on trial. Rosa got together a black congregation and they started to boycott against the Montgomery buses. The boycott lasted for 361 days and the buses were eventually desegrgated.Rosa Parks

A man by the name of Martin Luther King, Jr. who was also a civil rights participant during this era. Martin Luther King Jr, was running a campaign of peace in instances such as this. He wanted us to conduct our struggle on the highest plane of dignity and discipline. He didn't want us to meet physical force with physical force but with soul force. His belief was that two wrongs don't make a right and we as black people in a white world had to be above the rest.

In 1963 Martin Luther King gave his famous "I Have A Dream" speech from the steps of the Lincoln Memorial. This speech was very inspirational and it gave black people hope for good things to come. In this speech he made analogies of African-Americans getting written a bad check. "America has given the Negro people a bad check--- a check which has come back marked "insufficient funds." But we refuse to believe that the bank of justice is bankrupt." This is a very intriguing statement. He is saying that America has no hope and faith in black people, but we keep trying to prove them wrong. Some people might say that we were stupid for trying to prove them wrong but thought all of these mishaps we kept trying to move forward. While taking a class at UCI titled "The Psychology of a Black Man", the professor Thomas Parham once said to me that"African-American people believe in collective survival." At first I couldn't understand what that meant but the more I thought about it, the more sense it made. He was saying that as a nationality we are not selfish people. We don't believe in one person so call"making it." If we make it, we make it together as a nation. He wants to "lift our nation from the quicksands of racial injustice to solid rock of brotherhood."KING

Thurgood Marshall (1908-1993) is probably most noted for his work in the civil rights movement. He was the first African American to serve on the supreme court. Marshall served on the supreme court bench for 24 years and would have continued if his health would have permitted.

Before Marshall was appointed to the Supreme Court he worked hard in the legal field as an attorney and led a very interesting life. In the year 1930 Marshall graduated from Lincoln University (Cum Laude) and three years later in 1933, he earned his law degree from Harvard University. From 1940 to 1961 Marshall served as legal director for thee NAACP, along side Charles Hamilton (the first African American to win a case before the Supreme Court) designed long term goals to end the injustice of segregation in America. Marshall gained world wide attention and fame by arguing and winning the landmark court case of Brown v. Board of Education. The outcome of this particular case was it made the segregation of schools in America illegal.

The road to the Supreme Court Justice seat was a long and bumpy one to say the least. At the time he was nominated America was still a blatant racist country and the black man was looked down upon. Nominated by Lyndon Johnson in 1967, it took a lot of time to get the nomination push through. Before finally succeding Tom Clark, Marshall had already argued 32 cases and winning 29 of them.

Thurgood Marshall was and still is an exceptional man. He got into the system to overcome the system. He worked for African Americans all the way until his recent death on Jan 24, 1993. Thurgood Marshall can truly be said to be one of the many forefathers of the civil rights movement.

"Equal means getting the same thing, at the same time, at in same place."

Thurgood Marshall

George Washington Carver (1864-1943) was born a slave in Mississippi. Carver earned his high school Diploma after the Civil War working as a farm hand. He put his way through Iowa Agriculture college by assuming the duties of the school janitor. Shortly after graduating from Iowa Agriculturer college, Carver received his Masters degree from the same institution. With all of his work being monitored he was quickly given a position on the faculty. After a brief stint at Iowa, Carver left to join Booker T. Washington at Tuskegee Institute.

Carver was and is still world renowed for his achievements in the field of agriculture. He taught sharecroppers and farmers how to grow, preserve and maximize their yield in rotating crops. In 1914 Carver influenced southern congressman to move to other crops besides cotton crops that were being threatned by the "boll weevil" thus revolutionized southern agriculture. Carver developed processes for manufacturing paper, ink, shaving cream, lindeum, syntheic rubber, plastics, bleach, metal polish and over 300 consumer and industrial products from the peanut and sweet potato. Carver was sought throughout the world. Carver was sought out by people from Joseph Stalin to Mahatma Ghandi. Stalin sought Carvers opinion to help exploit the fertility of the huge mass of the former USSR. Stalin wanted to maximize the full output of the area and Carver had that sort of knowledge to maximize the areas potential output. Ghandi asked Carver to help build and maintain his countries agricutural system. In a effort to contribute to Africas need, Carver developed a peanut milk. The peanut milk is a nutricious alternative to the traditional cow's milk. By developing an alternative to cow's milk, Carver helped save the lives of hundred of babies in west Africa.

It is interesting that while at Tuskegee, Carver never took out a patent on any of his inventions. Carver was offered huge salaries to work for Thomas Edison and Henry Ford but choose instead to work for the improvement of the quality of life for the disinfranchished. Carver was a self-less person and only wanted the people to better off.

Paul Cauffee (1759-1817) was a Quaker ship-owner with roots traced back to African American and Native American Ancestry. Cauffee was an intense man that advocated getting free slaves back to Africa and helping them settle down. Cauffee was a smuggler of people back to their native land. Cauffe intended to make one trip per year at his own expense, taking the freed slaves to Africa and returning with cargo. He took thirty eight freed slaves to Freetown, Sierra Leone, but due to his untimely death in 1817 the freedom missions ceased.

Cauffee was also a good business man; you might even say he was a philanthropist. With the freedom missions came great expense, and Cauffee did pay for everything himself. What he did not pay for himself British contributed. With the British involvement he felt by them helping they were contributing to the regeneration of Africa. Cauffees' greatest accomplishment might have been gaining the right for African Americans to vote in the state of Massachuttes in 1783.
