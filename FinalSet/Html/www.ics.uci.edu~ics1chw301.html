  1. What is the function of Add-ons and is it a client or a server?
    In the design of web clients, they're usually designed to display HTML pages, and they display text files. Some clients are capable of displaying more files than other, but for the most party they can handle an extremely limited variety. To display other kinds of formatted files, or video, sound, and graphic files, you need a variety of external programs. Typically these are called viewers, although players, tools, and add-ons are other names for them. Essentially, the add-ons compensate for the browser's limitations, offering users a means of fully accessing every link they find on the web. Add-ons is a general term encompassing viewers, players, and other multimedia additions to web clients.

  2. What is HTML and how does this relate to the World Wide Web?
    HTML is a simplified derivation of SGML, or Standard Generalized Markup Language, which is a code used to make documents readable across a variety of platforms and software. HTML operates through a series of codes placed within a text document and these codes are translated by a World Wide Web client into specific kinds of formats to be displayed on the screen. These items include links, lists, headings, titles, images, forms and maps. The documents on the World Wide Web browsers are usually HTML documents. HTML carefully defines the structure of a document so that any browser can read it and display it in a way that is best for that browser.

  3. Who is Adam Smith and what kind of theory did he propose about the study of economics?
    Adam Smith is known to be the father of economics. He believes the "invisible hand" of self-interest leads individuals to promote the interests of others. The theorem of the Invisible Hand states that given a number of ideal conditions, optimizing behavior on the part of individuals and firms under pure competition leads to an efficient outcome. The art of economics consisits in looking not merely at the immediate but at the longer effects of any act or policy; making investment consists in tracing the consequences of that policy not merely for one group but for all groups.

  4. What is microeconomic and macroeconomics?
    Microeconomic is the branch of economics that studies the decisions of individual households and firms. Microeconomics also studies the way in which individual markets work and the detailed way in which regulation and taxes affect the allocation of labor and of goods and services. Macroeconomics is the branch of economics that studies the economy as a whole. It seeks to understand the big picture rather than the detailed individual choices. In particular, it studies the determination of the overall level of economic activity -- of unemployment, aggregate income, average prices, and inflation.
