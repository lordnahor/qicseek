  1. What is the World Wide Web?

     My answer is... The World Wide Web is a matrix of computer systems from around the world linked together to provide a wide variety of information. One distinguishing feature about the Web is that it uses the HTML markup language. This allows information from a variety of documents to be linked together in a continuous format. This ease of use is what makes the Web so popular, and the amount of information it is able to provide is growing at an astonishing rate everyday. Despite the vast amount of computer systems, Universal Resource Identifiers, or URLs, allow users to link up to the various servers. URIs are addresses of servers that provide specific information.

  4. What is Gopher and how does it differ from the Web and FTP?

     My answer is... The Gopher system is like the World Wide Web in that it can access the same information using various servers such as FTP, telnet, or WAIS. However, the method of display in Gopher is very different from the World Wide Web. Although the Gopher also uses hyperlink documents, it does not utilize the hypertext enviroment. For example, if one connects to the Gopher Jewels Server, one would find a set of menus with links to other documents. FTP, on the other hand, does not utilize any form of hyperlink technology. Rather, it is a protocol for directly transferring files. A list of Anonymous FTP sites contains sites for a broad range of information.

  5. How are pain and touch signals transmitted to the brain for processing?

     My answer is... The most simple model of sensory information transmission can be found in the anatomy of a frog. The Free Nerve Endings and Meissner's Corpuscles detect pain, temperature, and light touch. Each touch receptor is specialized for specific types of signals. The touch receptors transform the signals into electrical neural signals which travel to special parts of the brain for processing. This transformation of enviromental energy into electrical energy is called transduction, and this electrical energy travel through cutnaeous nerves, which line the deepest skin layers. Thus, sensory information is transduced into electrical signals, which travel through nerves to the brain.
