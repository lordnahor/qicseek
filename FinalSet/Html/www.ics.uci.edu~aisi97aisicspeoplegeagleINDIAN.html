MANDAN

Here are some interesting facts on my heritage:

  • The Three Affiliated Tribes-the Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara- stay on the Fort Berthold Reservation.
  • The Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara were established in the Dakotas between 1500-1700.
  • Maize, squash, beans, pumpkin, tobacco, and sunflowers were primary crops among the Mandan.
  • The Mandan speak a Siouan dialect.
  • The village of Good Fur Robe had occupied the present day site of Mandan in 1738. The original residents journeyed here from the Gulf of Mexico or the South Atlantic Coast.
  • The Mandan were peaceful people and survived off the land.
  • The principal source of food and clothing was the buffalo.
  • The tribe held a festival to honor the buffalo every year. This would mark the season for the buffalo's return.
  • The Mandan lived in permanent earth lodge villages for centuries before the coming of the white man.
  • Mandan and Hidatsa were considered the 'farmers, merchants, and bankers' of the northern plains.
  • After the death of a family member, a Mandan would mourn for a year.
  • In 1804, President Thomas Jefferson commissioned William Clark and Meriwether Lewis to explore the Missouri River and find a route to the Pacific Ocean by the Columbia River.
  • The Mandan lived on the Missouri River between the Heart River and the Little Missouri Rivers, N.D.
  • The Mandan became historically famous when Lewis and Clark chose the site of the Mandan settlement to build the first winter camp in 1804-05. Fort Mandan is named after the Indians who befriended the expedition party and it was built on the banks of the Missouri River.
  • The Fort Laramie Treaty of 1851 established the Fort Berthold Reservation for the Mandan, Hidatsa, and Arikara. The Fort Berthold Reservation is located in central North Dakota. The west and south segments of the reservation is rough grassland and "badlands."
  • The Fort Laramie Treaty established a large area of land described vaguely as the entire right bank of the Missouri River from the mouth of the Yellowstone River and from the mouth of the Powder River to the Heart River. This piece of land included parts of Wyoming, Montana, North Dakota and South Dakota. The area was named to honor an American Fur Company founder, Bartholomay Berthold.
  • Between 1851 and 1891, on executive orders and congressional acts changed the size of the reservation from 13,500,000 acres to 930,000 acres. In 1954, the reservation lost 152,300 acres of land to the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers for the filling of the Garrison Reservoir.
  • 1937 marks the last of the small pox epidemics. This epidemic resulted in population decline of the Mandan to the point where it could no longer function as an independent unit.
