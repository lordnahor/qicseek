    1. What is the World Wide Web?
      In March of 1989, Tim Berners-Lee stated in his proposal, WorldWideWeb: Proposal for a Hypertext Project,<">a way which allows a user to follow links pointing from one piece of information to another one.<"> These hypertext links were originally developed by Geneva's European Particle Physics Laboratory, or CERN, as an easier way for researchers to share their information. It took over a year to get off the ground, but the World Wide Web has expanded many times over since 1993.

      At over one million computers and growing, the Internet is the world's largest computer newtwork. Sifting through, and finding information on the World Wide Web can be overwhelming, to say the least. There are many places to start on a search for Web information, but I have found two particularly helpful ones to begin with. The first is called the World Wide Web Worm. The Worm is located on the WWW server of the University of Colorado homepage. Its function is to search throughout the Web for home pages.     4. What tools can I use to search for information on the Internet?
      There are two main tools to guide a user through the Internet: Gopher and the World Wide Web. Gopher provides a menu listing of subjects. When one selects an item on a Gopher menu, another menu of related items is, in turn, displayed. This proceedure is repeated until the user finds the information he/she is looking for. The World Wide Web gives the user a graphical interface from which to make their selections, making it a more user-friendly environment.

    5. How can the World Wide Web benefit me as a drama major?
      The World Wide Web has much to offer a drama student, whether it be through the online magazine Broadway World Wide, or Portland State University's academic program. Broadway World Wide offers theatre listings for both Broadway and off-Broadway shows, a help wanted link which displays jobs currently available in the New York theatre community, and also up to date news about the world of the stage. All of these things benefit me by keeping me abreast of what is going on inside and outside of the university setting, enabling me to establish contacts for my future career in theatre. Portland University's site gives a guided tour through the college and its theatre program, including some facts about the state of Oregon. This is very helpful, as I am planning on moving there in the near future.
