Introduction:

In 1981, the Corporation introduced the Microsoft Disk Operating System ("MS-DOS") for the PC. This operating system is designed to be used with a specific PC microprocessor , the "brain" of the computer which controls the operation of a computer by coordinating the interaction between the computer's microprocessor, memory, and peripherals (i.e. attatched devices such as monitors, disk drives, and printers.) In a sense, the PC operating system functions as the "central nervous system" of the computer. The computer cannot function without it. Most of the computers in the world today utilize the designed x86 class of microprocessors. These x86 microprocessors do not only include Intel manufactured microprocessors (286, 386, 486, and Pentium) but also include microprocessors manufactured by other firms that are based on a similar architecture. MS-DOS is the mainstream operating system used for these Intel and Intel-based microprocessors. In addition to the operating system, applications are required for the computer to perform any useful task. Applications are software programs that communicate with the operating system to allow the user to perform a variety of functions. Application programs include word processors, spreadsheets, and database programs. Each application is written for a particular operating system and incorporates the codes that allow the application to function within the operating system environment. Microsoft manufactures and markets a number of its own very successful applications.

In 1985 Microsoft introduced a graphical user interface operating system called Windows. Windows allows the user to give commands to the operating system by pointing and clicking on a device called a "mouse" eliminating the need for the user to memorize complex commands. In addition to simplifying the instruction process. Windows also allows the user to run more than one application at one time. To date, Microsoft estimates that over 50 million computers use Windows.

The marketing of the MS_DOS and Windows operating systems is one primarily through original equipment manufacturers ("OEMs"). OEMs manufacture PC's and sells them to consumers and businesses either directly or through resellers. Some of the large OEMs would be IBM , Compaq , and Dell , all of which have agreements with Microsoft. The MS-DOS operating system is generally distributed only to OEMs, with upgrades being offered to consumers. Microsoft sold 80 percent of its Windows units through OEMs.

The Complaint:

The complaint from the Antitrust division of the Department of Justice listed certain points that concerned them as to Microsoft's domination of the market for operating systems. First of all, the Department of Justice defined the relavant market and the monopoly power that Microsoft held. Second, the Department of Justice listed the barriers to entry by new firms. Third, the Dapartment of Justice stated the reasons why. Finally, the Department of Justice listed the anticompetitive effects of Microsoft's behavior.

The relavant market was defined as the market for personal computer operating systems using the x86 class of microprocessors with the relavant geographic market being the world. It is improtant to note that operating systems written for other microprocessors will not work on machines with the x86 class microprocessor, therefore OEMs who sell and customers who buy x86 machines cannot use other operating systems. Since at least the mid-1980's, Microsoft has had a monopoly power in the relavant market due to their ability to maintain an extremely high market share in excess of 70 percent with MS-DOS installed on over 120 million PCs and Windows installed on over 50 million PCs worldwide.

The next concern of the Department of Justice was the barriers to entry that faced new firms. The first barrier to entry being the considrable time and expense required to develop, test, and market a new operating system. Other barriers that face software developers are: 1) the absence of a variety of high quality applications that will run on a new operating system and the difficulty in convincing independant software vendors ("ISVs") to develop those applications. 2) The lack of a sizable installed base of users, and 3) The difficulty in convincing OEMs to offer and promote a non-Microsoft PC operating system, particularly one with a small user base and relatively few applications availible to run in it. Because the value of an operating system to the consumer lies on the availibility of a variety of high quality applications that run on the system, and the number of users who are able to use and share information without additional training, these barriers to entry magnify and reinforce each other. Due to this reasoning, software manufacturers tend to develop applications for operating systems with a large installed user base, and consumers tend to purchase operating systems with a large availibility of applications.

The third concern of the Department of Justice was the anticometitive nature of Microsoft's marketing agreements. The "per processor" licenses that Microsoft was issuing requires that OEMs pay a royalty for each computer the OEM sells containing a particular processor whether or not the OEM has installed a Microsoft operating system in that computer or not. Because the profit margins in the computer industry are currently very thin, the OEM has the incentive to enter into these agreements with the reasoning being the ability to purchase the operating system at the lowest possible cost. OEMs believe that they must install MS-DOS and Windows in their computers because of Microsoft's monopoly position in the marketplace. Because the "per processor" contract penalizes the OEMs for inststalling a non-Microsoft operating system. OEMs bound by the "per processor contract" are deterred from using sny competitive alternatives nto the Microsoft operating systems. Another feature of the marketing agreements between Microsoft and the OEMs is the long term nature of these agreements. Microsoft requires minimum commitments and credits unused balances to future contracts, which effectively lengthens the contract, even though the frequent contract duration of three years or more exceeds the product life cycle of most operating system products. The credit to future contracts make the OEM wary of installing a non-Microsoft operating system. Microsoft also often extends the term of the OEM licenses to beyond five years.

By blocking acess to OEMs, Microsoft has taken away from competitors a critical point of entry. The majority of PCs are sold through the large OEMs, all of which have "per processor" agreements with Microsoft. Even if a competing operating system were to license itself to a smaller OEM that it not covered by Microsoft's "per processor" agreement, it would not be able to achieve any commercial success. Thus a competing operating system must have access to large OEMs.

Another type of agreement that Microsoft enters into are agreements with Independant Software Vendors ("ISVs"). ISVs develop applications and Microsoft has sought to restrict the ability of important ISVs to develop applications for competing operating systems by pursuing non-disclosure agreements. The terms of these non-disclosure agreements prevent major ISV developers from working with other operating system developers and competing technologies for long periods of time. ISVs enter into these agreements because they need access to Microsoft's beta tests with new products especially with the upcoming version of Windows, code named Chicago, that will be released sometime in 1995. The beta tests are conducted before the commercial release of the produuct so the ISVs can obtain critical information about the interfaces in the operating system that connect with applicartions - information which is necessary to write applications for that operating system. It is important for Microsoft to conduct these "beta tests" not only for ISVs but for themselves as well. For Microsoft, the "beta tests" provide them with important feedback about the operating system. Also, the sucess of the operating system depends on the availibility of applications and providing the ISVs with the "beta tests" insures that there can be a large availibility.

Finally, the Department of Justice listed the effects of Microsoft's anticompetitive behavior. According to the Department of Justice, Microsoft engaged in unlawful acts and practices that harmed competition, consumers, and innovation. First of all, Microsoft contracts have excluded competitors on a basis other than the merits of their product. Second, Microsoft impedes the ability of rival operating systems to compete in the market by making it more difficult for competitors to convince OEMs to install their operating systems on their computers and to convince consumers that the system is a possible alternative to MS-DOS and Windows. Third, the exclusionary license policy that Microsoft uses deprives competing operating systems of sales they might have been able to secure had they been able to develop a sufficient installation base through the OEM channel.

The damage to competition by Microsoft's actions also harms consumers. Consumers that want a choice of operating systems may be forced to pay more since the OEM will have to pay a double royalty, one to Microsoft under the 'per processor' agreement and one to the competing operating systems manufacturer. Thus Microsoft has limited the choices that consumers have with operating systems and applications.

The Economics:


The reason why Microsoft can possibly gain monopoly power despite the availibility of competing products is because of the existence of network externalities. Network externalities can be defined as the benefits a product has as it emerges as a standard due to having a large user base. In other words, the value of a particular product becomes higher as more users adopt products with the same technology. The fast pace of technological change in computers and computer software has made consumers of one technology dependent on other technologies. In the PC operating systems market, the success of a particular product is dependent on the complementary products availible that are compatible with that particular operating system. Incompatibility creates a problem for consumers and competitors if network externalities exist by harming the ability of the user of a certain PC operating system to "swap" information with the user of the mainstream PC operating system as a well as preventing the user of the non-mainstream operating system from accessing the complementary applications and other resources of the mainstream PC operating system.

Initial consumer demand hinges on the decision of the consumer to buy or not to buy the operating system. However, the consumer usually would not make his choice when he purchases the computer. The choice of the consumer will have an effect on purchases in future generations since the consumer will most likely demand a future improved unit later. Thus, the 'per processor' agreements with the major OEMs changes the popularity of Microsoft's MS-DOS and Windows operating system into a de facto standard by giving the OEM every incentive to install a Microsoft operating system and not to install the operating system of its competitors since the OEM must pay Microsoft regardless of whether or not it installed Microsoft's operating system on a computer. This leads to the consumer automatically having a Microsoft operating system when they purchase the computer, thereby causing them to be more likely to upgrade their Microsoft product in future generations, whereas prices aside, the success of a PC operating system would otherwise depend on the technical capabilities of the product and whether or not future generations adopt the same PC operating system as well as the network effect.

Though standardization by network effects benefits the consumer by setting technological standards for the consumer, it also hurts the consumer by eventually limiting their choices despite development of superior non-standardized products. Furthermore, standardization created by Microsoft's agreements with the OEMs has a negative effect on competition in general. Through it's agreements, Microsoft capitalized on a temporary popularity of its products and created a standard by using every method possible to deny their competitors the same sales outlets.

The Response:

Though the complaint filed against Microsoft is well supported by the arguments made by the Department of Justice and by economic theories, certain legal conditions must be met in order to warant actions against Microsoft. The Department of Justice asserts that Microsoft's marketing agreements violate Section 2 of the Sherman Act. Microsoft's response to these claims has been to deny any wrongdoing and to claim insufficient grounds for an antitrust action. Even many individuals outside of Microsoft that are familiar with the Personal Computer industry fail to see the basis for any action against Microsoft.

Section 2 of the Sherman Act "prohibits monopolists from acquiring or maintaining power by blatantly illegal or uncompetitive acts." This means that the market power a company develops must be the result of natural growth and that power cannot be used improperly to restrain competition. In order for a company to be seenn as using its market power improperly, the Department of Justice must show that Microsoft has the willful intent to monopolize and the power to do so. The problem with this is that there is no clear definition of monopoly power in Microsoft's case because the market structure has been blurred by the Department of Justice's establishment of numerous software segments (i.e. operating systems, applications, etc.) Furthermore any monopoly power that Microsoft might have could be argued as being the result of the insight of Bill Gates and the ability of his company to produce technologically superior products. The lack of clarity in PC software market share also weakens the Department of Justice ability to show intent through leveraging, because it would require that the Department of Justice have strong evidence of high market share.

Microsoft has complained that this action by the Department of Justice is aimed at taking away the success of an innovative company. Cponcerning the "per processor" agreements with the OEMs, executives at Microsoft have insisted that these are optional bulk discounts and that Microsoft does not force OEMs to enter into these agreements. Also Microsoft executives point out that consumers have equal access to competing products on the market as they do with Microsoft products.

The Final Settlement


On August 19, 1994, the Department of Justice and the Microsoft Corporation submitted a settlement agreement to Federal Judge Stanley Sporkin for approval. In the settlement agreement, the Microsoft Corporation agreed to change the 'per processor' agreements in to 'per system' agreements and reduce the length of its contracts to terms of one year. Finally, the company agreed to limit the use of non-disclosure agreements for developers of its operating system products and permit computer manufacturers to renegotiate contract terms if they pleased.

Why He Said No..


Federal Judge Stanley Sporkin refused to approve the settlement agreement between the Department of Justice and the Microsoft Corporation for a number of reasons. First, Judge Sporkin found that the Department of Justice did not provide the court with enough information for it to make a proper public interest determination. Second, he found the scope of the settlement to be too narrow. Third, he found that both the Department of Justice and the Microsoft Corporation have been unwilling to adequately address certain anticompetitive practices, and finally he found the settlement to be ineffective as a remedy for the complaint in that the mechanisms incorporated in the settlement for enforcement and compliance were unsatisfactory.
