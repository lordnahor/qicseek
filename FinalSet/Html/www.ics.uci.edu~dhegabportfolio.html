Research Methods Infographics
The purpose of this interactive narrative-based tutorial on statistical methods is to establish a simplified way for students to learn statistical concepts (specifically Hypothesis testing with t-tests) that are grounded in real-life examples. In addition to a narrative, an interface is presented that allows students to interact with data sets that are accessible on-line.

The goal is to give students interested in doing research a fun way to quickly and clearly understand critical concepts in statistics, needed to continue to experiment with their own data sets (or online ones) so they can interpret future data sets they work with in meaningful and statistically sound ways.

The online application is available below! Feel free to click through Steps 1-4 on the middle left to learn more about hypothesis testing with sample data sets!

Usability design methods included ideation of narrative of tutorial and design of front-facing interface screen to help students interpret data sets.

Our team designed and prototyped an interactive graphic novel game with researcher characters engaging in discovery of publishing in different disciplines (and contrasting research tracks/tenure tracks amongst those disciplines). Our team added to the narratives and backstories of each character by using information gathered by interviews with UCI faculty in the disciplines represented in our game.

Lastly, each character/academic world was represented in a different time period and using a different game modality (i.e. point-and-click, visual novel game, text based adventure, etc.).

This website project was created to help undergraduate/graduate students identify multi-disciplinary Human-Computer Interaction academic programs in the U.S. (this addresses the problem of finding HCI programs typically listed under different disciplines such as computer science, information sciences, design, psychology/human factors, etc.). This website will also include pertinent information about HCI resources such as online UX courses, industry associations/events, HCI research labs, and relevant conferences in the U.S.

Some usability research methodologies used in this project include: wireframing, ideation, rapid prototyping, initial sketches of the webpage(s), and user studies to facilitate iterations of the project.
Many research methodologies in HCI are overlapping, while others are entirely disparate. These infographics (which are still works in progress) hope to identify research methods that are connected, while revealing bigger classification systems for which many can be put to use. The hope is to clarify relationships between these methodologies so they can be used more synchronously.
