The Questionable Web

As stated above, I have an interest in how the World Wide Web will be shaped in the future. Originally its design was structured around scientific information, but its recent developments in handling graphics has restructured if not opened up opportuniti es for a variety of art-based sites. One way to enter this virtual gallery is to go to on of the many sites that specialize in art. In fact many Universities and galleries have their own camp us exhibitions on display such as The Electric Gallery. For those who are interested in posting their own art or videos, Otis offers an e asy way to do this. Your work becomes world wide and feedback can be e-mailed directly to the artist. This type of electronic resume might put food on the plate of the starving artist. Alas the Web offers the last uncensored wall to paint on.

If the Web can offer economic potential to the Artist it can also offer commercial applications for any field. So far many banks and businesses have placed adds upon the Web. You can visit The Commerce Net. Here you can find information on many major corporations, a brochure on a bank or company such as IBM. With better security for handling credit card numbers and personal checks, a electronic mall can offer exclusiv e products and services to the Web community. MCA Cyberwalk is just one of the many virtual malls that you can visit. Here one might buy that hard to get Weezer T-shirt or download the latest info on their favorite band .

What good is the World Wide Web if it can't entertain? Like most who enter the Web it is very easy to get distracted from your original goals by the many cool sites that exist. In fact the WWW has changed the way of looking at information via computers. For me, entertainment comes in films (especially Pulp Fiction) Film is my biggest love. The Hollyweb On-line Film Guide can tell me what films are in development, reviews as well as a myriad of trivial information. The WWW also brings you a variety of magazines and newspapers into your computer from Mother Jones to Playboy. Soon books might even get published electronically on the Web. In fact, you can now search for book titles stored on the net. If unsure on were to go, visit Yahoo, they have many different types of entertainment sites on the Web to chose from.

Above I mentioned Yahoo A site that can help you navigate your way through the anarchistic Web. Still to fully strain all the juice from this orange different services and search engines were created, and continually pop up though out the WWW, each with a different task in mind. Lycos (tm) is the catalog of the Internet. The The Lycos web explorer, developed at Carnegie Mellon Universi ty, searches the World Wide Web every day (including Gopher and FTP space), building a database of all the web pages it finds. Another popular search engine is EINet Galaxy. Here also topics are organized under categories such as Arts and Humanities, Law and Recreation. During the d ay this site can get busy thus slowing down your searches. Both Einet and Lycos allow one to put their own information in one of the categories. COMMA uses forms to assist wi th searches. This type of engine is common on many sites and helps with a more exact query. And then there is Netsearch. NetSearch is a unique tool for gathering widely dispersed information from the Internet. The NetSearch Project allows simultaneous querying of a wide array of remote databases, plus immediate assimilation of results into a highly readable format. Basically it does everything but look under the kitchen sink. I deem these engines as the most important part of the Web. Kinda like a road map for the information impaired W3 traveler.

One might view the WWW as a lazy library, a system that constantly spews out information for a viewer who never leaves the seat. Still, does all this information educate? I find that most of the subjects that I research on the WWW were opinionated as w ell as educational (minus the human environment). It raises issues on our changing interactions with society. Can classes one day be taught through the WWW such as illustrated by the Virtual Summer Sch ool Project? I don't think society is ready to hang up human interaction so quickly. Instead the Web might become a way to supplement your education. Working to enhance not replace, sites such as The Smithsonian of fer many different subject matter on-line. This On-line Teaching and Learning will most likely become the standard in education. Schools such as The Online University ,advertising on the WWW, hope to reach and unite students who are geographically separated from each other and the campus. The price of this education will probably rise with an increase in popularity, bu t then again ,who can put a price on an education?
