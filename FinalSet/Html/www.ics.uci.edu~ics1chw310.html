  1. What does W3 define?

    W3, or the World Wide Web stands for a variety of things. According to T. Berners-Lee, one of the things W3 stands for is the concept that one can retrieve an array of information which have references by which they could be retrieved.

    The World Wide Web also has an address system (URI) which is incorporated so that one has a listing of the references, although many different protocols exist.

    There is also a network protocol (HTTP) which gives the performance and features which are not otherwise available. For a detailed definition of what HTTP is, click Hypertext Transfer Protocol.

    The WEB also uses a markup language (HTML), which serves the purpose of transmitting basic things across the net. For a more detailed definition of what HTML is, click Hypertext Markup Language.

  2. What are the two main types of Internet connections and what are the differences between the two?

    The two main types of Internet connections are     directand indirect connections. Both types use either modems or existing newtwork cards, and have a wide range of prices. The main difference between the two is that with a direct connection, the computer acts as an individual node on the Internet.

    With a direct connection, the computer has its own IP address, and it can be established as a server for FTP, Gopher, News, or the W3. By doing this, you can use the software available to bring in mail to your own computer. For programs such as Mosaic, one must use direct connections.

    Indirect connection, however, gives a disk space and access time on another computer. The mail received and transfer files stays on that server.

  4. How many medical schools are there in the state of California? Which of these are within the University of California and where are they located?

    At this moment, there are thirteen medical schools existing in the state of California. Of these thirteen, five medical schools are within the University of California. The five medical schools which are located within the University of California are at Davis, Los Angeles, San Francisco, San Diego, and Irvine.
