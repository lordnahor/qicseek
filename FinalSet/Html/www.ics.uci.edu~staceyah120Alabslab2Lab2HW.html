The Monty Hall Problem

Consider again the Monty Hall problem described in this lab.
  1. Write an R function to simulate one play of the Monty Hall game where you do decide to switch doors. The function should return TRUE (or 1) if you win the car and FALSE (or 0) if you do not win the car. Note that we are assuming if Monty has a choice of which door to open, he will choose one of the doors randomly (e.g., if he needs to choose between doors 2 and 3, each door has an equal probability of being the one Monty chooses).
  2. Use your R function from part a. inside a for loop to simulate 10,000 plays of the game. Calculate the empirical probability on winning the car when using the switching strategy (i.e., in what proportion of the 10,000 games did you win the car?)
  3. Does your simulation suggest that it is a better strategy to switch doors than to stick with your original choice? Explain.

Hints

Disclaimer: These hints are for the way I envisioned the function, but there are many other correct ways to simulate playing the game.

  • When writing the R function to simulate one play of the game, you can assume without loss of generality that you initially choose door number 1. Then have the function randomly choose one of the three doors to have the car behind it. (That is, instead of thinking that the car, goat and goat are fixed and that you randomly choose a door, view the game as the chosen door is fixed and the car, goat and goat are randomly assigned to the three doors.)
  • You will use a sequence of "if" statements to decide which of doors 2 and 3 Monty opens (assuming you chose door 1).
  • Remember that the logical operator "==" will check if two things are equal and will return a TRUE if they are, and a FALSE if they are not.
  • You will again get plenty of use out of the "sample" function. If you don't remember how to use this function, type "?sample" to open the help file.
