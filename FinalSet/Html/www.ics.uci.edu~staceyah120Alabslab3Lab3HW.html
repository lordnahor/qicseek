Lab 3: Assignment

You must type your lab assignments and submit them through the EEE Dropbox tool. A dropbox will be set up for each of the four lab assignments. This lab assignment is due Wednesday, Nov. 13 by 5pm. You must include all relevant R code used to answer the questions.


  1. Look up the help file for the Hypergeometric distribution in R. The Hypergeometric distribution as stated in the back of our textbook is defined in terms of parameters A, B, and n. R, on the other hand, defines the Hypergeometric distribution in terms of parameters m, n, and k. Express each parameter from the book (A, B, and n) in terms of the parameters used by R (m, n, and k).
  2. Recall the scenario from Exercise 8 in Section 3.1 of our book (Problem #2 on Homework 4). Define X as the number of red balls obtained from a sample of size 20 with replacement from a certain box where 10 percent of the balls are red. Use the built-in probability functions in R to calculate the following probabilities:
    1. P(X = 3)
    2. P(X <= 3)
    3. P(X > 3)
    4. P(X >= 3)
  3. The Central Limit Theorem (Theorem 6.3.1) is a famous result that is the basis of a large number of statistical methods, and we will prove this theorem in Chapter 6. For now, you are going to perform a simulation that will demonstrate the results of this theorem. Your simulation should do the following:
    1. Choose a probability distribution from which to generate data. (This can be any of the probability distributions introduced in this lab, but do not choose the normal distribution.)
    2. Generate 1000 random samples of size n = 5 from your chosen distribution (use a for loop!). For each sample, calculate the sample mean (using the "mean" function in R), so in the end, you have a vector of 1000 sample means.
    3. Create a histogram of the 1000 sample means with a density plot overlaid.
    4. Repeat parts b. and c. for random samples of size n = 10, n = 25, and n = 100. Write a few sentences describing what happens to the distributions of sample means as the sample size increases -- as n increases, what happens to the shape of the histogram? the spread/variability of the sample means? the center/mean of the sample means?
