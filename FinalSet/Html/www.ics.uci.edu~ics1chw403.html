Introduction

The study of anthropology is not often considered outside of its known context of human culture and evolution. Most people can tell you that anthropology deals with the exotic ways of the worlds people, that anthropology is a sort of record of different ethnic and cultural groups, and that anthropologists also study human evolution. It is because of this latter aspect of the field that people often comment to the effect of "oh anthropology, so are you going to go digging in the deserts of Africa?" And while archaeology is, in fact, a facet of anthropology, they are not one in the same. In fact, anthropology is actually such a wide-ranging field that at times it becomes difficult to classify.

In an introduction to anthropology course, one is often taught that the discipline can be generally divided into four categories. These are cultural (includes social anthropology-these are sometimes seperate categories) anthropology, physical or biological anthropology, archaeology, and linguistics. Here, cultural anthropology is meant to define the study of various cultures and their unique characteristics, both cultural and social. Physical anthropology is the study of human evolution. Archaeology defines the systematic recovery and scientific study of material evidence remaining from past human cultures, and linguistics encompasses the study of human languages including grammer and technical relationships. However useful these categories may be, I believe it will soon become evident that anthropology is actually much farther reaching.

In this paper I would like to present an overview of many of the more specialized fields within the discipline and focus on one of them in particular-ethnomusicology. This will not be an exhaustive look at the different subcategories of anthropology, but instead, will be designed to give an idea as to the vastness of the field.

An Overview

The specialized fields of anthropology are, in general, extensions of the four categories mentioned above. However, the more detailed or limited in scope a specialized field becomes, the more it seems to combine not only these different categories but also it begins to incorporate different disciplines entirely. The World-Wide Web Virtual Library anthropology section gives a good idea as to the depth that anthropological specialized fields contain.

Beginning with an example which would loosely be placed under the category cultural anthropology, we can look at Russian and East European Studies . This extensive study covers cultural, political, social, and linguistic issues. For instance, one can take a "virtual museum tour" of the Treasures of the Czars, look at population statistics, or look at a map of the Commonwealth of Independent States. Also, there are links to political issues such as the Armenian Reasearch Center which covers Armenian history, genocide, and the current stuggle in Nagorno-Karabagh. The linguistics resource area covers many Russian and East European languages such as Estonian, Slovenian, and Russian. Clearly, this study combines at least three of the categories mentioned above.

Likewise, Asian Studies programs are also broadening. One can find extensive information concerning the Asian continent, language and literature, art, and Asian religions including Islam and Buddhism. A very modern area of research in cultural anthropology concerns gangs. I found an interesting document in this area entitled Life in a Vietnamese Gang. This study began as a film at the University of Southern California and is about a 20 year old Vietnamese guy named Richy Phan who gets involved with a gang here in Orange County. I found this document particularly interesting as it includes gang poetry and a letter from Richy Phan in jail. Also, this study is a good example of contemporary cultural anthropology mixing traditional techniques with film.

Linguistics is the one subcategory of anthropology that is the most straight forward. It is generally studied by linguists, although anthropologists frequently collect the information, and is usually restricted to the study of one language although there are also comparative studies. At the same time, human evolution, which is just one of the subcategories of anthropology, has itself many facets. Under the area of Evolution in the WWW Virtual Library anthropology section several of these facets are listed. From this document human evolution seems to cover not only the study of primate and human development across time, but also includes biochemistry, molecular biology, genetics, and ecology to mention a few. So it seems that human evolution, like so many other areas of anthropology, combines with other disciplines in its studies.

Although the examples above give an idea as to the incorporated subcategories and disciplines that anthropological studies entail, it is easier to get an idea of the multiple areas of anthropology by visiting a few of these specialized or limited areas of study.

  • Ethnoarchaeology and Ethnohistory is an interesting field dealing with the archaeology and history of specific ethnic groups. This site includes documents which are ethnic historical and documents on the interpretation of specific archaeological finds.
  • The Australian Indigenous Populations Study is a sight that incorporates traditional cultural anthropology such as social control and cultural resilience with other displines such as law (aboriginal land rights), history, and fine arts.

Ethnomusicology

Ethnomusicology is a specialized field of anthropology which combines cultural anthropology and music. This limited area of study is unique in that it is not offered at many universities and in that it requires some background knowledge in the elements of music, such as pitch, rhythm, melody, and harmony. The anthropological aspect of this area comes into play with the collection of data. Anthropologists, in this case ethnomusicologists, will collect the relevant data (music) by using a classic cultural anthropology technique called field study. With a field study, the researcher will actually physically go to the place of interest (ex. Iran), stay for a fair period of time (often 6 months to 2 years), and collect information by personal interview. During this time the researcher will learn the language and cultural features of the given country, become friendly with the people, and conduct his/her investigative study.

Studies in ethnomusicology try to study and understand the music of different ethnic groups in the context of that group's cultural characeristics and traits. They ask questions such as, for instance, "what element of African tribal culture makes their music abundant in slow beating drum rythms?" Although the focus of these types of studies are on the music rather than the culture, the culture is, of course, an influential factor in that music.

Studies in ethnomusicology have been conducted on many different countries and ethnic groups within those countries (ex. studies on Jewish music within Turkish culture). For example, studies have been done on the music of Latin America, Subsaharan Africa, and India. These studies often focus on the non-Western elements of the music and compare these elements to Western standards. Also, these type of studies often focus on the religious music of the culture and its influence on the popular style. In fact, studies in ethnomusicology have amounted steadily and there is now a Web site called EthnoForum/hypermedia that deals exclusively with ethnomusicology.

Since ethnomusicology is such an unusual and concentrated area of study, it is best to browse through one of these studies to get an idea as to their content and form. An excellent comprehensive example of an ethnomusicological study is entitled The Burmese Piano Music of V KO KO and was done by Prof. Robert Garfias here at UCI. This study includes historical context, musical style and framework, and the characteristics of that specific cultures musical facets. It is well worth it to visit this site and gain an understanding as to the fundamentals of ethnomusicology research.

Conclusion

I think that anthropology has been affected positively by the World-Wide Web. It has given the field an avenue to public awareness and it has given anthropologists a way to stay on top of recent research. In conclusion, anthropology was not always such a wide ranging area of study. If fact, at its inseption it was a fairly narrow field, limited to matters of human evolution and traditional cultural studies. However, anthropology is now a discipline which has not only broadened in scope in recent years, but continues to grow.
