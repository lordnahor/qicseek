  1. What is Alzheimer's disease and how is it caused?

     Alzheimer's disease is the most common form of dementia that occurs in the elderly. 5-10% of the population over the age of 65 show some sign of the disease. Alzheimer's disease can also affect other ages. Some patients may have symptoms as early as the fifth decade of life. People with Alzheimer's disease suffer from a progressive deterioration in cognitive performance. Typical symptoms also include confusion and forgetfulness.

    Alzheimer's exact causes are still unknown. However, current research has found evidence to isolate individuals who are at high risk to the disease; one of them being heredity. Others also at risk are a small portion of the population who possess mutations in the amyloid protein precursor or APP protein localized on chromosome 21. A large number of the population suffering from the disease are associated with a locus on chromosome 14. But because Alzheimer's disease is common in this region, its precise disease gene can not yet be determined. Most Alzheimer's patients have been found to possess one or two copies of the EA allele of the apolipoprotein E gene found on chromosome 19. Persons with the same background are at severe risk to the disease. Environmental risks such as head traumas and aluminum toxicity have also been identified. One cause proposal, known as the cholinergic hypothesis, suggests that the reduction in cognitive levels can be caused by a deficiency in cholinergic neurotransmission.

    More information about Alzheimer's disease can be found at the Alzheimer Web Home Page.

  2. What is the subject of Paleontology about?

     Paleontology is a vastly diverse field, divulged in the fascinating pursuit of seeing the "long and interesting past and even more intriguing and hopeful future". It's much much more than the study of fossils. It is the progress of discovering the ecologies of the past, seeing new ideas and facts about evolution, and finding out about where we, as humans, belong in this world---all through the study of fossils. It is phenomenally diverse in the way it pools its repository of knowledge from several different areas, such as: biology, geology, ecology, anthropology, archeology, and, yes, even computer science to analyze and comprehend the ways the world has evolved and will continue to evolve.

  4. What is a URL?

     URL is an acronym for Uniform Resource Locator. It is the manner in which documents or data available on the World Wide Web are addressed or referenced. A URL specifies 4 things:
    1. the agreed upon standard method for transferring the data, known as the protocol
    2. the internet name of the site or server providing the requested resource
    3. the internet port number
    4. the location of the resource in the server's directory structure

    URL's look like this:

    • http://info.cern.ch:80/default.html
    • ftp://wuarchive.wustle.edu/mirrors/msdos/grapchics/gifkit.zip
    • telnet://netcom.com

    The protocols seen in the listed examples are http, ftp, and telnet. Other protocols not seen include: gopher, WAIS searches. News, and Mailto. The portions after the colon and two slashes indicate the name of the servers; info.cern.ch, wuarchive.wustl.edu, and Netcom.com in the listed examples. Only the first example specifies a port number; 80 in its case. If no port number is specified, as in the last two seen examples, then it is defaulted to 0. The final portions of the URL is the path of the object. In our second example /mirrors/msdos/grapchics/ is the path with gifkit.zip as the desired resource. If no path is given, then the URL defaults to the root directory as the path. URL's with a specified port number may have already an understood path associated with it. And if no resource or object is given, the available resources in the path are listed for the viewer's selection---this is as far as I know with http only; I've not been able to try it out the circumstances with other protocols.

  5. What are some neat inlined image and icons I can use to spice up my HTML?

    This seems to be a popular topic. Even as I was working on this document, I've already been approached by at least half a dozen people asking. Here's two ways I've found from reading the WWW FAQ file I found locally thru a link in the ICS 1C homepage and remotely through various other places---I must have ran into this link about a dozen times from different home pages in one sitting.

    1. Load the html page you see the image you like. Switch on the Load to Local Disk option if you have it. Then select the Reload Images. You will then be prompted for names of the image files as they arrive and await to be saved. In my own attempts as this, the X version of Mosaic that I use seems to want to only save the html document and no images. Perhaps it's just an error with my current version. Try it yourself with on your own Mosaic copy/version or other browser, whatever it may be.
    2. Find out the URL of the image you like by viewing the source of its HTML document. Then open do an "Open URL" on it. Your add-on that you use to view images with your browswer will display it for you. And if it's not one of those cheesy ones, it'll permit you to save the image to your local disk.

    The other (brute force) method is to hunt down these images yourself tediously by looking at public archives of pictures and icons. Here are a couple neat WWW achieves laid out especially for inlined HTML documents: Fuzzy's Icon Directory, NCSA's Icon Archive, Moby's Icon Archive , Eric's Graphic Archive, Realm Graphics, Daniel's Icon Archive, and Rutger's Icons.
