International trade in Russia

International business is truly a great potential business in our modern world. The total volume of world trade today is around $7 trillion each year. Regardless of a country's resources, no country can produce all of the goods and services demanded by its citizens. For people of Russia, they have to stand in long lines for necessary daily products because the country's lack of resources. Thus, the Russian state's economic policies try to integrate the country into a prosperous economy by opening its doors to foriegn trade activities.Foreign investments are considered to be the main source of money to restructure the former socialist economy. Doing international business is extrememly risky, a company should understand the legal aspects in foreign investment, which kind of managerial style is best for the company and what are the government regulations being imposed upon the business. These are the questions we need to consider when attempting to extend businesses abroad.

Business Management
The success of any firm depends largely on how well that firm is managed. And to manage a firm well, managers must plan, organize, lead and control. What makes international business so challenging is the fact that these basic responsibilities are much more difficult to accomplish in an international context. It is best to find licensed broker to operate in Russian market. Brokers are licensed and governed by the Ministry of Finace of Russia;therefore, they can guide companies through any confusions and problems. The brokers offer safe-keeping services to their clients. The brokers offer a nominee ownership registration and settlement of transactions on behalf of a client; however, there are difficulties in the maintenance of monetary accounts with brokers. According to the Russian legislation only banks have an official right to keep monetary accounts and organize monetary settlement for all types of transactions (Rinaco, 1994). Companies can choose between two ways of carrying out their investment activities in Russia. They can either establish a registered permanent representative office or a branch in Russia, or work without such an office or branch. The second option is that operations in Russia are done either through the intermediary organization or through the authorized person.

But first a firm must decide how much involvement they want their business in the country. Because different levels of involvement in international business require different kinds of organizational structure.

  • Independent Agents
  • licensing
  • Branch Offices
  • Strategic Alliances and Franchising
  • Direct Investment
  • Global Business
The most recommended one is strategic alliances and franchising and sometimes it is called joint ventures. An international strategic alliance is a foreign company that finds a partner in the country who would like to begin a business together. Instead of just licensing this partner, each party to the alliance agrees to put an approximately equal amount of resources and capital into a new business. The parent company supplies the business expertise, business knowledge, and brand name. Local investors supply the capital, local contacts, and understanding of the local market.

Government Regulation
Government regulates business for variety of reasons, the two most important of which are to protect competition and to meet the nation's social goals. According to Russian's foreign person, legal or natural, has the right to invest in Russian enterprises, to buy or to sell Russian securities and repatriate profit from Russia after payment of appropriate taxes. Nevertheless, legislation governing foreign investment is undeveloped and exists in a very primitive and basic form (Rinaco, 1994). There is a tendency for a large number of violations and infringements to occur when the governemnt try to carry out the liberalization of foreign economic activities. There might be practices of illegal competition, and failure to return the specified earning into the country from export operations. It is easy to acknowledge that establish capitalism in Russia is not easy. Indeed, the list of potential problems are staggering. They lack all or most of the following:

  • Laws that protect private property and guide the establishemnt of contracts.
  • Standardized accounting procedures
  • Banks
  • Stock and bond markets
  • Convertible currency(currency that can be exchanged for the currencies of other countries)
Moreover, continued political and economic turmoil in Russia has led to a pattern in which many internal laws, tariff regulations, and restrictions on foreign trade are here today and gone tomorrow (Ebert, 1994).

Strategies for Success
Success in exporting doesn't come overnight. But following a few simple steps can certainly help to increase a firm's chances. Experts suggest the following plan for businesses that want to start exporting (Griffin, 1994).

1.Establish export potential. To export successfully, firms need to know how well their products will stack up against competitors. The U.S. Commerce Department helps companies obtain this information through its Export Qualifier Program. The program provides a computer analysis of a company's organizational and product strengths and compares those factors to characteristics associated with successful exporters.

2.Develop a marketing plan. Once export potential is established, exporters must develop a marketing plan. Research about culture, customs, language, and currency is essential.

3.Arrange financing. International business investments usually requires a large start-up cost. Arranging for loans to finance foreign ventures was a major stumbling block for many new companies. Many state agencies can help businesses obtain loans to finance international business activities.

4.Assemble a distribution system. Shipping and distrubuting to foreign markets can be complicated. Some companies work successfully with distributors and agents in foreign countries. Another option is using an international export management company or freight forwarding company that will handle all the paperwork and keep its customers advised.

5.Protect Revenues. Transferring money internationally involves letters of credit and bank drafts. To minimize risk on the international front, firms should avoid selling in countries experiencing political or social upheaval.
