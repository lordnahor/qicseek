    1. What are the three main aspects that characterize the World-Wide Web? The three main characteristics of the World-Wide Web are hypertext, multimedia, and the Internet. Hypertext refers to the kind of documents found on the Web. They are basically the same as plain text documents with the added feature of what Vannevar Bush called "selection by association." With a hypertext document there are connections within the text to other related documents. Multimedia simply refers to the Web's capability to incorporate graphics, sound, video, and animation in it's documents. The Internet is the global mass of cables and computers that make the World-Wide Web possible by providing the physical links between the network of computers that make up the Web.

    2. What is the "client-server architecture" as it relates to the World-Wide Web? The client-server architecture is the overall plan by which different computers interact within the World-Wide Web schema. The "client" aspect is actually a computer program or browser which runs on a computer operated by a human user. The "server" is a computer with a program that runs all the time and contains some information. The client contacts the server with a request for information. In response, the server provides the information to the client and the human is happy. The client-server architecture can also be characterized as an "agent" relationship because a server of one client can in turn be a client to another server.

    3. What is anthropology and why would someone major in it? Anthropology is, in very general terms, the study of mankind. This study can include theory of human origin and evolution, called physical anthropology, human cultural development, called cultural anthropoloy, as well as the study of human social customs and beliefs, called social anthropology. There are actually many branches of anthropology which extend into more specific and limited areas, such as linguistics and archeology. As to why one would choose to major in the subject, it is difficult to say. Perhaps one enters into anthropology after being enthralled by a lecture from one of the greats, Richard Leakey for instance. Another reason why someone might choose anthropology as a major might be that after extensive world travel one realizes his/her intensive interests in the specifics of the world's various cultures and simply must learn more.

    4. Who is Richard Leakey and what does he have to do with anthropology? Richard Leakey is the son of Louis and Mary Leakey whose pioneering archaeological field work in the eastern regions of Africa has contributed fundamentally to the field of physical anthropology. Richard Leakey has continued the work of his parents and is currently the director of the National Museum of Kenya. He has written several books concerning the evolution of our species and its possible future, the most recent of which is entitled Origins. Mr. Leakey is not only in the forefront of archaeology and physical anthropology but also contributes to animal protection and preservation in East Africa. He spoke on this latter issue last year when he gave a lecture at the Bren Events Center.

    5. What are some jobs that people in anthropology pursue? Majoring in anthropology allows one to consider entering many different areas in pursuit of a career choice. I suppose the most obvious extension of attaining an undergraduate degree in anthropology is to continue one's studies in the field by attending graduate school. This route will most likely lead to a life of teaching and occasional field studies. One could also use the humanistic background provided by an anthropological education to enter fields such as law, psychology, communications, or social work.
